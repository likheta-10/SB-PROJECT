Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a broad, multidisciplinary field of computer science dedicated to creating systems capable of performing tasks that typically require human intelligence. These tasks include visual perception, speech recognition, decision-making, and natural language translation. The conceptual origin of AI dates back to the mid-20th century, famously anchored by Alan Turing’s question, "Can machines think?" Today, AI has evolved from a theoretical pursuit into a transformative technology that underpins modern software, shifting the paradigm from rigid, rule-based programming to systems that can adapt and respond to complex environments.

The field of AI is generally divided into two main categories: Narrow (or Weak) AI and General (or Strong) AI. Narrow AI refers to systems designed and trained for a specific, limited task—such as filtering email spam, recommending movies, or playing chess. These systems can execute their defined task with incredible precision, often surpassing human capabilities, but they lack the ability to apply their intelligence to any context outside their programming. In contrast, Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) remains a theoretical goal. AGI would describe a machine possessing the ability to understand, learn, and apply knowledge across any intellectual domain, mirroring the flexible, cross-disciplinary cognitive abilities of a human being.

To achieve intelligent behavior, AI systems rely on a variety of core techniques, most notably symbolic AI and machine learning. Early AI development heavily featured symbolic AI, also known as "Good Old-Fashioned AI" (GOFAI), which relied on explicit logic, if-then rules, and curated knowledge bases to solve problems. While highly effective for structured tasks like expert medical diagnostic systems or chess engines, symbolic AI struggled with the messy, chaotic nature of the real world. Modern AI has largely pivoted toward connectionist approaches and machine learning, where neural networks learn directly from massive datasets, allowing the software to generalize and recognize patterns without needing explicit programming for every possible edge case.

Natural Language Processing (NLP) and Computer Vision are two of the most prominent domains where AI has achieved human-like capability. NLP focuses on the interaction between computers and human language, training models to analyze sentiment, summarize text, and generate coherent prose. Computer Vision, on the other hand, enables machines to interpret and process visual information from the world, such as identifying objects in digital images or tracking pedestrians in self-driving car feeds. Together, these fields allow AI to bridge the gap between digital processing and human perception, transforming unstructured sensory inputs into structured, actionable data.

As AI systems become deeply integrated into critical sectors like healthcare, finance, and criminal justice, the study of AI ethics and alignment has become paramount. Because these models learn from historical human data, they risk absorbing, amplifying, and institutionalizing systemic biases present in those datasets, leading to unfair or discriminatory automated decisions. Furthermore, the "black box" problem—where complex deep learning networks make accurate decisions but human developers cannot trace the exact logic behind them—raises serious transparency and accountability concerns. Ensuring that AI remains safe, ethical, and aligned with human values is currently one of the most critical challenges facing researchers and policymakers globally.
